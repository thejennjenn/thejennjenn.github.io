Free Gift? Where?!
Entincing new users to make a purchase on their first visit

Problem

There is a low rate of purchases made by first-time customers. New customers who do not make a purchase within their first several uses of the app tend to abandon it.

Goal

How might we entice and encourage new customers to make a purchase when using the app for the first time?

My Role/ Results??

Research causes of cart abandonment. Identify areas of opportunity and issues with existing sign-up flow. Brainstorm strategies to reach goal and designed low and high fidelity mocks. Iterate design based on data and user feedback.

BACKGROUND

Wish- Shopping Made Fun! is a shopping mall in your pocket that provides customers with relevant and affordable products not found elsewhere.

Wish's target demographic are trendy bargain hunters in the low to mid level income range. As an ecommerce app, Wish thrives on purchase transactions. However, we noticed that there is a low 10% conversation rate for new users who purchase a product when using the app for the first time. Based on past user data, there are several assumptions as to why this might be true: users are not interested in the products, users lack a sense of trust, or friction in the payment process. With several assumptions to investigate, we decided to experiment with reducing friction in the payment process to see its impact on new user purchase completes.

RESEARCH

After reading several user experience articles on payment, asking users about their thoughts on entering information/ payment process, and doing a competitive analysis of the pros and cons of successful and popular ecommerce platforms, we came to the conclusion that when checking out,

there is friction and the inconvenience of entering your shipping and credit information when paying.

However, when all that information is already present, assuming that we've already aquired that information from a past purchase, customers can easily “Place Order” without thinking twice about their decision. Although Wish does save that information once after a customer makes a purchase, (as shown below)...

Above: The current checkout process inside the app. If users have previously checked out, their shipping and payment information are saved allowing users to easily "Place Order" without entering any further information. But how might we get that information before a new customer decides to checkout?

...how might we obtain a customer’s shipping and credit information even earlier in order to make the purchasing experience effortless when checking out as a new user?

APPROACH

We saw opportunity in taking advantage of the sign-up process. The existing sign-up flow asks customers to enter their name, email, and password (or sign-up with facebook), and to select their birthday, gender, and interested categories in order to offer relevant products.

Above: Current sign-up flow in order from left to right before the redesign.

Although the rule of thumb is to make the sign-up process quick, simple, and frictionless, we will take the risk of asking users for a little more information in this area. The assumption is that if we are able to obtain a user’s payment and shipping information before they start shopping, it would reduce the friction of checking out since all that information would already be present for them to "Place Order." Of course, this comes with the cost of losing users at sign up. Therefore, how might we reduce the friction of asking for more information while still adding value?

We started off by identifying issues with the existing sign up flow.

Above: Mood graph of a user's process through the sign-up flow.

Testing with 5 co-workers who identified with our target demographic, we tracked an average user’s mood as they progressed through the sign-up process. Participants were tested individually and were shown each screen one-by-one. They were asked to mark their mood levels from good mood (smiley face) to bad mood (sad face) according to each screen. After the test, participants were asked to explain their mood level for each screen.

The results averaged an obvious decline in mood when users were asked to enter confidential information like birthday and gender, which we use to offer relevant products. Knowing that our users were not a fan of the gender/birthday section, we needed to find a subtle way of gathering that information, while also obtaining a user's shipping/ billing information!
